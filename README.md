# Improving Housing Affordability in Nova Scotia: Rental Supply or First Time Homebuyer Incentives?

## Decision Statement

Should the Director of Housing Policy at the Nova Scotia Department of Municipal Affairs and Housing prioritize rental supply expansion or first time homebuyer incentives over the next 3–5 years to improve housing affordability in Nova Scotia?

## Executive Summary

Housing affordability has become a major challenge in Nova Scotia, driven by population growth, limited housing supply, and rising housing costs. Renters are facing low vacancy rates and increasing rents, while many first time homebuyers are struggling to enter the market due to high prices. These pressures have created a complex policy environment where government action plays a significant role in shaping housing outcomes.
To address affordability concerns, policymakers commonly consider two strategies, expanding rental housing supply or offering incentives to first time homebuyers. Increasing rental supply may help relieve pressure in the rental market by improving availability and stabilizing rents, but it often requires significant investment and time before results are realized. In contrast, first time homebuyer incentives can improve access to ownership in the short term, yet they may also increase demand in a constrained housing market, potentially contributing to higher prices.
This project evaluates which of these approaches would more effectively improve housing affordability in Nova Scotia over a three to five year period. By examining housing and demographic trends and considering the broader system dynamics, the analysis aims to support evidence based decision making for provincial housing policy.

## Milestone 2: Data Sources Summary

This analysis uses publicly available Canadian housing data focused on affordability and tenure differences between renters and homeowners. The 30% income threshold is widely used in housing policy as a benchmark for affordability stress, while core housing need captures households that experience inadequate, unsuitable, or unaffordable housing conditions. These variables directly inform the decision of whether policy should prioritize increasing rental supply or supporting first time homebuyers to improve overall housing affordability.

## System Archetype Identification

![Image 2026-03-23 at 2 45 PM](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/dd96696f-fe23-42b3-909e-c834fa746f7d)

The housing affordability system in Nova Scotia reflects the Limits to Growth archetype, where increasing demand creates pressure that is eventually constrained by limited system capacity. In this context, growth is driven by rising rental demand and increasing core housing need, as more households are unable to access homeownership and remain in the rental market. This demand places upward pressure on rental prices, which further increases affordability stress, measured by the proportion of households spending more than 30% of their income on housing. This dynamic is captured in the reinforcing loop (R1), where higher prices and affordability pressure continue to drive demand within the rental system. However, this growth is constrained by limited rental housing supply, which acts as the system’s key bottleneck. The balancing loop (B1) represents the response to this constraint, where higher prices incentivize construction and increase rental supply, eventually improving vacancy rates and stabilizing prices. Despite this, delays in construction and limited investment reduce the effectiveness of this balancing response. Evidence from the data supports this pattern, as rental prices have consistently increased while vacancy rates remain low, and a significant share of households exceed the 30% affordability threshold. Together, these dynamics demonstrate a system where growth is persistently constrained by supply limitations, confirming the presence of the Limits to Growth archetype.

## Status Quo Scenario (No Policy Change)

If no significant policy changes are implemented, the housing system in Nova Scotia is expected to continue its current trajectory over the next 5–10 years, with the reinforcing loop (R1) remaining dominant. Increasing rental demand, driven by population growth and limited access to homeownership, will continue to push rental prices upward. As prices rise, a growing proportion of households will exceed the 30% income threshold for housing, increasing core housing needs and further reinforcing demand within the rental market. Although the balancing loop (B1), which represents rental supply expansion, will still be present, its effects will remain limited due to delays in construction and insufficient scale of new developments. Vacancy rates are likely to remain low, potentially around 1–2%, sustaining upward pressure on prices and limiting affordability improvements. Over time, this dynamic will result in worsening affordability conditions, with more households experiencing financial strain. A key uncertainty in this scenario is the rate of population growth, which could intensify demand pressures if it accelerates. Additionally, unintended consequences may include increased housing insecurity and greater reliance on government support systems. Overall, without intervention, the system remains constrained by supply limitations, allowing reinforcing pressures to dominate and drive continued affordability challenges.

## Intervention A: Rental Supply Expansion

If policymakers prioritize rental supply expansion, the system begins to shift over the next 5–10 years as the balancing loop (B1) becomes more influential. Increased investment in rental construction and supportive policies will gradually expand housing supply, leading to higher vacancy rates and reduced pressure on rental prices. As vacancy rates increase from approximately 1–2% toward more balanced levels of 3–5%, competition among landlords will help stabilize or slow rental price growth. This shift weakens the reinforcing loop (R1), as slower price increases reduce affordability pressure and limit the growth of core housing need. Over time, this can lead to a more balanced system where supply better aligns with demand, improving overall housing affordability. However, this scenario is subject to several uncertainties. The most significant challenge is the time delay between policy implementation and the completion of new housing units, meaning benefits may not be realized immediately. Additionally, construction constraints such as labor shortages, rising material costs, and regulatory barriers may limit the pace of supply expansion. Potential unintended consequences include overbuilding in certain segments of the market or uneven distribution of new housing. Despite these risks, this intervention addresses the system’s core constraint and offers the most sustainable long-term improvement.

## Intervention B: First-Time Homebuyer Incentives

If policymakers focus on first-time homebuyer incentives, the system evolves differently over the next 5–10 years, with the reinforcing loop (R1) remaining largely intact. While these incentives may help some households transition into homeownership, they primarily act on the demand side of the housing market without addressing the underlying supply constraint. As more individuals are enabled to enter the housing market, demand for homes increases, which can drive up housing prices. This can indirectly impact the rental market by maintaining high rental demand, as many households remain priced out of ownership. As a result, rental prices may continue to rise, and the proportion of households spending more than 30% of their income on housing may remain elevated or increase. The balancing loop (B1) is minimally affected in this scenario, as rental supply does not significantly increase. A key uncertainty is the extent to which these incentives translate into meaningful increases in homeownership rates. Unintended consequences may include further price inflation in both ownership and rental markets, as increased demand is not matched by supply. Overall, this intervention may provide short-term relief for some households but does not resolve the structural issues driving affordability challenges.

## Leverage Point Analysis

The most effective leverage point in this system is increasing rental housing supply capacity, particularly through policies that accelerate construction and reduce development barriers. This point offers high impact because it targets the system’s core constraints being limited supply, rather than addressing symptoms like demand. By expanding supply, the system becomes more capable of absorbing growing demand without significantly increasing prices. This intervention directly affects both key feedback loops. Increasing supply strengthens the balancing loop (B1), as higher vacancy rates help stabilize or reduce rental prices. At the same time, it weakens the reinforcing loop (R1) by reducing affordability pressure and slowing the growth of core housing needs. Because this leverage point changes the structure of the system, it creates more sustainable long-term outcomes compared to demand-side policies. However, there are risks and potential resistance. Time delays between policy implementation and completed housing may limit short-term impact. Additionally, zoning restrictions, political opposition, and rising construction costs may slow development. Despite these challenges, targeting rental supply remains the most effective intervention, as it addresses the root cause of housing affordability issues and improves system balance over time.

## Connection to Decision-Maker

The analysis suggests that the most effective decision for policymakers in Nova Scotia is to prioritize rental supply expansion over demand-side interventions. The scenario analysis shows that maintaining the status quo allows the reinforcing loop (R1) to continue driving affordability challenges, while demand-focused policies such as first-time homebuyer incentives fail to address the system’s underlying constraint. In contrast, increasing rental supply strengthens the balancing loop (B1), improves vacancy rates, and stabilizes rental prices over time. Although this approach involves delays and implementation challenges, it directly targets the root cause of the affordability crisis. As a result, the decision-maker should focus on policies that accelerate rental construction and reduce development barriers, as this offers the most sustainable and system-wide improvement in housing affordability.

## Final CLD Explanation

The final causal loop diagram illustrates how housing affordability in Nova Scotia is shaped by the interaction between rental prices, affordability pressure, housing need, demand, and supply-side responses. The system is driven by two key feedback loops: a reinforcing loop (R1) and a balancing loop (B1), which together explain why affordability challenges persist over time. The reinforcing loop, labeled R1: Affordability Pressure Reinforcing Loop, shows how rising rental prices increase the percentage of households spending more than 30% of their income on housing. As affordability pressure increases, more households fall into core housing needs. This leads to greater reliance on the rental market, increasing rental demand. Higher demand then places additional upward pressure on rental prices, causing the cycle to reinforce itself. This loop explains how affordability issues can continue to worsen over time, even without major external changes, as pressure within the rental system builds on itself. The balancing loop, labeled B1: Rental Supply Expansion Balancing Loop, represents the system’s stabilizing response. As affordability pressure and core housing need increase, there is greater pressure for government intervention. In response, government housing policy supports increased rental construction activity, which expands rental housing supply. As supply increases, vacancy rates improve, which reduces pressure on rental prices. Lower rental prices then reduce affordability stress and core housing needs. This loop is balanced because it works against the reinforcing loop by stabilizing prices and improving overall housing conditions. The structure of the system shows that affordability challenges are largely driven by strong demand combined with limited supply. While the reinforcing loop can intensify affordability pressure quickly, the balancing loop operates more slowly due to delays in construction and policy implementation. This time lag is important because it means improvements in affordability may take time to materialize. The key leverage point in the system is rental housing supply capacity, particularly through policies that accelerate construction and reduce development barriers. Strengthening this part of the system directly improves the effectiveness of the balancing loop while weakening the reinforcing loop. This connects directly to the decision-maker’s choice, as prioritizing rental supply expansion is more likely to produce long-term improvements in housing affordability than demand-side policies such as first-time homebuyer incentives.

## Decision Recommendations

The recommended course of action is for the Director of Housing Policy at the Nova Scotia Department of Municipal Affairs and Housing to prioritize rental supply expansion over first-time homebuyer incentives over the next three to five years. Based on the data analysis and system dynamics, expanding rental supply is the more effective approach because it directly addresses the root cause of housing affordability challenges rather than only providing short-term relief. The evidence from the analysis consistently shows that renters face significantly higher levels of housing stress than homeowners. A large proportion of renter households spend more than 30% of their income on housing, and renters are more likely to experience core housing needs. At the same time, low vacancy rates and rising rental prices indicate that demand for rental housing is exceeding available supply. The causal loop diagram further supports this, showing a reinforcing cycle where rising rental prices increase affordability pressure, which contributes to greater housing need and sustained rental demand. Without intervention, this cycle is likely to continue worsening affordability conditions. Prioritizing rental supply expansion directly targets this imbalance. Policies that support increased construction, reduce regulatory barriers, and encourage higher-density housing can expand the overall supply of rental units. As supply increases, vacancy rates are expected to improve, which can help stabilize or reduce rental price growth over time. This approach not only reduces affordability pressure but also weakens the reinforcing cycle that drives housing stress. In contrast, first-time homebuyer incentives primarily increase demand without significantly expanding supply, which may unintentionally place additional upward pressure on housing prices. There are, however, important uncertainties to consider. The most significant limitation of a supply-focused strategy is the time required for new housing developments to be completed. Delays related to construction timelines, labour shortages, rising material costs, and regulatory processes may slow the impact of these policies. Additionally, if population growth accelerates beyond expectations, demand pressures may continue to outpace supply increases. In such cases, the effectiveness of rental supply expansion may be reduced in the short term. To move forward, several practical steps should be considered. The province should streamline approval processes to reduce development delays, update zoning policies to allow for more multi-unit housing, and provide targeted incentives to encourage rental construction. Collaboration with municipalities and private developers will also be important to ensure that supply expansion is both feasible and aligned with local needs. Further analysis could strengthen this recommendation by incorporating more detailed projections of construction timelines, vacancy rate trends, and regional differences within Nova Scotia. Despite these uncertainties, the current evidence clearly supports prioritizing rental supply expansion as the most effective and sustainable strategy for improving housing affordability.
